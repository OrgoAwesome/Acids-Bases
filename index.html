Understanding Acid-Base Strength

SMALLER ENERGY GAP = STRONGER ACID/BASE

Acid and base strength depends on the energy gap between reactants and products.

Acid Diagrams

Neutral Acids (HA)

SMALLER ENERGY GAP = STRONGER ACID

  • "Factors that destabilize HA make it easier to lose H⁺, creating a stronger acid"
  • "Factors that stabilize A⁻ make H⁺ loss more favorable, creating a stronger acid"

Summary: All 5 factors work by either raising HA's energy or lowering A⁻'s energy

Charged Acids (HX⁺)

SMALLER ENERGY GAP = STRONGER ACID

  • "Factors that destabilize HX⁺ make it easier to lose H⁺, creating a stronger acid"
  • "Factors that stabilize X make H⁺ loss more favorable, creating a stronger acid"

Important: For charged acids, trends are often reversed! Smaller atoms and less electronegative atoms make stronger acids

Base Diagrams

Neutral Bases (B)

SMALLER ENERGY GAP = STRONGER BASE

  • "Factors that stabilize B increase its electron availability, creating a stronger base"
  • "Factors that destabilize BH⁺ make H⁺ more easily accepted, creating a stronger base"

Summary: All 5 factors work by either lowering B's energy or raising BH⁺'s energy

Charged Bases (X⁻)

SMALLER ENERGY GAP = STRONGER BASE

  • "Factors that destabilize X⁻ increase its reactivity, creating a stronger base"
  • "Factors that stabilize HX make H⁺ addition more favorable, creating a stronger base"

Important: For charged bases, destabilizing X⁻ increases its reactivity with H⁺

Acid Strength / Stability of Conjugate Base (A⁻)
Factor 1: Size +
Rule: Larger atoms form stronger acids (when comparing atoms in the same group)
Examples:
  • HI > HBr > HCl > HF (increasing acidity down the group)
  • Why? Larger atoms (like I, Br) better distribute and stabilize the ⊖ charge in A⁻
  • H₃O⊕ > NH₄⊕ (H₃O⊕ is more acidic)
  • Why? ⊕ charges are more stable on smaller atoms (like O vs N)

Remember: ⊖ are more stable on LARGER atoms, ⊕ are more stable on SMALLER atoms

Factor 2: Electronegativity +
Rule: More electronegative atoms form stronger acids (when comparing atoms in the same period)
Examples:
  • HF > H₂O > NH₃ > CH₄ (increasing acidity across the period)
  • Why? More electronegative atoms (like F, O) better stabilize the ⊖ charge in A⁻
  • H₃O⊕ > NH₄⊕ (H₃O⊕ is more acidic)
  • Why? For charged acids, ⊕ charges are generally more stable on less electronegative atoms, but the smaller size of O compared to N is the dominant factor here

Remember: ⊖ are more stable on MORE electronegative atoms, ⊕ are more stable on LESS electronegative atoms

Factor 3: Inductive Effects +
Rule: Inductive effects can either RAISE or LOWER acidity:
  • Electron-withdrawing groups (EWGs, like F, Cl, CF₃) will stabilize ⊖ and increase acidity
  • Electron-donating groups (EDGs, like CH₃, alkyl groups) will stabilize ⊕ and decrease acidity
Examples:
  • CF₃COOH > CH₃COOH (EWG increases acidity)
  • Cl₃CCOOH > Cl₂CHCOOH > ClCH₂COOH > CH₃COOH
  • Why? EWGs (like CF₃, Cl) pull electron density away, helping stabilize ⊖ in A⁻
  • R₃NH⊕ < R₂NH₂⊕ < RNH₃⊕ < NH₄⊕ (NH₄⊕ is most acidic)
  • Why? Alkyl groups (EDGs like CH₃) stabilize ⊕ charge, making a weaker acid
Factor 4: Resonance Effects +
Rule: Resonance effects can either RAISE or LOWER acidity:
  • Delocalizing a ⊖ in the conjugate base INCREASES acidity
  • Delocalizing a ⊕ in the acid REDUCES acidity
Examples:
  • Carboxylic acids (pKa ≈ 5) > Alcohols (pKa ≈ 16)
  • Phenols (pKa ≈ 10) > Cyclohexanol (pKa ≈ 18)
  • Why? Resonance spreads out the ⊖ charge in A⁻ (like in carboxylate), stabilizing it
  • R-C=O-H⊕-R > R-C=O-O⊖-R
  • Why? Delocalizing the ⊕ charge in the acid makes it less acidic
Factor 5: Hybridization +
Rule: Higher s-character leads to stronger acids
Examples:
  • sp (50% s) > sp² (33% s) > sp³ (25% s)
  • HC≡CH (pKa ≈ 25) > H₂C=CH₂ (pKa ≈ 44) > H₃C-CH₃ (pKa ≈ 50)
  • Why? Higher s-character means electrons are held closer to the nucleus, stabilizing the ⊖ charge better
Base Strength / Stability of Conjugate Acid (BH⁺)
Factor 1: Size +
Rule: Smaller atoms form stronger bases (when comparing atoms in the same group)
Examples:
  • NH₃ > PH₃ (NH₃ is more basic)
  • Why? Smaller atoms (like N vs P) concentrate electron density, making the lone pair more available to share
  • OH⊖ > SH⊖ (OH⊖ is more basic)
  • Why? ⊖ charges are less stable on smaller atoms (like O vs S), making them more reactive

Remember: ⊖ are more stable on LARGER atoms, ⊕ are more stable on SMALLER atoms

Factor 2: Electronegativity +
Rule: Less electronegative atoms form stronger bases (when comparing atoms in the same period)
Examples:
  • NH₃ > H₂O > HF (NH₃ is more basic)
  • Why? Less electronegative atoms (like N vs O) hold their electrons less tightly, making them more available to share
  • CH₃⊖ > NH₂⊖ > OH⊖ (CH₃⊖ is more basic)
  • Why? ⊖ charges are less stable on less electronegative atoms (like C vs N), making them more reactive and therefore stronger bases

Remember: ⊖ are more stable on MORE EN atoms, ⊕ are more stable on LESS EN atoms

Factor 3: Inductive Effects +
Rule: Inductive effects can either RAISE or LOWER basicity:
  • Electron-donating groups (EDGs, like CH₃, alkyl groups) will increase basicity
  • Electron-withdrawing groups (EWGs, like F, CF₃) will decrease basicity
Examples:
  • (CH₃)₃N > (CH₃)₂NH > CH₃NH₂ > NH₃ (increasing basicity)
  • Why? EDGs (like CH₃) push electron density toward the basic site, making the lone pair more available to share
  • CH₃CH₂NH₂ > NH₃ > CF₃CH₂NH₂ (CF₃ decreases basicity)
  • Why? EWGs (like CF₃) pull electron density away from the basic site, making the lone pair less available
Factor 4: Resonance Effects +
Rule: Resonance effects can either RAISE or LOWER basicity:
  • Resonance delocalization of the lone pair DECREASES basicity
  • Resonance stabilization of the conjugate acid (BH⊕) INCREASES basicity
Examples:
  • Piperidine > Pyridine (pyridine is less basic)
  • Why? Aromatic resonance in pyridine delocalizes the lone pair, making it less available
  • CH₃NH₂ > CH₃C(=O)NH₂ (amide is less basic)
  • Why? Resonance in amide delocalizes the lone pair between N and C=O, making it less available
  • R-C=O-O⊖-R > R-C=O-H⊕-R (in terms of basicity)
  • Why? Resonance delocalization of ⊖ charge in carboxylate makes it less basic
Factor 5: Hybridization +
Rule: Lower s-character leads to stronger bases
Examples:
  • sp³ (25% s) > sp² (33% s) > sp (50% s)
  • CH₃NH₂ (sp³) > CH₂=NH (sp²) > HC≡N (sp)
  • Why? Lower s-character means electrons are held farther from the nucleus, making them more available to share

Applying These Rules: Decision Flowchart

1 Identify your task:

  • Finding the strongest acid in a group of molecules
  • Finding the strongest base in a group of molecules
  • Identifying the most acidic proton in a molecule
  • Identifying the most basic site in a molecule
  • Determining conjugate acid/base
  • Predicting reaction direction
↓

2 For acid-base comparisons:

  • If possible, look up pKa values (lower pKa = stronger acid)
  • When pKa values aren't available, apply the 5 factors
  • Remember that factors may conflict - resonance and inductive effects often dominate
↓

3 For finding most acidic proton:

  • Identify all protons that could potentially be acidic
  • Compare their approximate pKa values from related compounds
  • Consider functional group trends: RCOOH (pKa~5) > RSH (pKa~10) > ROH (pKa~16) > RC≡CH (pKa~25) > RNH₂ (pKa~38) > RC=CH₂ (pKa~44) > RCH₃ (pKa~50)
↓

4 For finding most basic site:

  • Identify all atoms with lone pairs (typically N, O, S)
  • Compare their likely basicity based on the 5 factors
  • Consider functional group trends: R₃N > R₂NH > RNH₂ > NH₃ > ROH > H₂O > RCOOH
↓

5 For determining conjugate acid/base:

  1. Conjugate acid: Add H⁺ to the most basic site (atom with most available lone pair)
  2. Conjugate base: Remove H⁺ from the most acidic site (typically forms most stable anion)
↓

6 For predicting reaction direction:

HA + B ⇌ A⁻ + HB⁺

  • Reaction favors formation of the weaker acid and base
  • Compare pKa values: Reaction goes from LOW pKa to HIGH pKa
  • If pKa values are similar, equilibrium will be more balanced

In Short: Quick Reference Guide

Terminology

HA: Neutral acid

A⁻: Conjugate base of a neutral acid

HX⁺: Charged acid

X: Conjugate base of a charged acid

B: Base

BH⁺: Conjugate acid of a base

To Find Strongest Acid

HA → A⁻ + H⁺
?
Ask yourself: What stabilizes A⁻?

Look for:

  • Larger atoms (down a group)
  • More electronegative atoms (across a period)
  • Electron-withdrawing groups
  • Higher s-character
  • Resonance that stabilizes A⁻
More stable A⁻ = Stronger acid

For Charged Acids

HX⁺ → X + H⁺
?
Ask yourself: What destabilizes HX⁺?

Look for:

  • Smaller atoms (up a group)
  • Less electronegative atoms (across a period)
  • Electron-withdrawing groups
  • No resonance stabilization of HX⁺
Less stable HX⁺ = Stronger acid

To Find Strongest Base

B + H⁺ → BH⁺
?
Ask yourself: What destabilizes BH⁺?

Look for:

  • Smaller atoms (up a group)
  • Less electronegative atoms (across a period)
  • Electron-donating groups
  • Lower s-character
  • No resonance delocalization of lone pair
Less stable BH⁺ = Stronger base
